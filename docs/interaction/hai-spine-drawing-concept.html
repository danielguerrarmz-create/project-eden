the core loop

Draw. Infer. Gate. Resolve. Refine.

One continuous motion from the person's side — draw, watch, maybe redirect, watch again — even though the engine changes character completely at each stage. Getting the gate right is the whole difference between a collaborator and a guessing black box.

01

Draw

A single stroke across the 3D site.
Engine shows only a raw ink trace — no color, no guess.

02

Infer

Nothing — the engine's alone.
The stroke read as one proposal: arch / aperture / rib, drawn as a ghost.

03

Gate

Nothing, by default.
A ~500ms window to redirect. Silence accepts. It never blocks.

04

Resolve

Watches.
Position holds; only surface hardens — wireframe to matte to grain, ~2s.

05

Refine

Grabs a node and pulls.
The full sculpt vocabulary — same object, no mode switch.

The gate never blocks

No dialog, no confirm. The ghost is already resolving the instant the window opens. The gate is time to redirect, not a gatekeeper standing in front of the result.

Silence is acceptance

Do nothing for the window and the ghost finishes becoming what it proposed. You walk through the gate by continuing to exist on the other side of it, not by unlocking it.

Redirect is drawing

If the read is off, you do not hunt for undo. You start drawing again. A new stroke discards the ghost. The one path that must feel instant is the correction path.

the spine vocabulary

A spine is the intent, not a profile.

Three kinds, told apart by how the stroke behaves. Each answers the same hard question a different way: how does a flat gesture become a 3D thing without ever asking "which plane are you drawing on?"

open air

Arch

The centerline of an arch, drawn as its own silhouette — foot to crown to foot. The spine is the arch's visible arc, not a side view of it.

Commits to 3D by inferring a vertical plane from the ground-contact chord — the same "infer a plane, project the pointer" trick the cage handles already use, run once at draw time.

on surface

Aperture

A closed loop marking where material opens rather than where a member runs. How you find an oculus by drawing one, instead of scrolling one open on a ring that already existed.

Almost always drawn on the live shell, so every point is already a raycast hit against real 3D geometry. No plane to infer — the surface supplies the third dimension.

on surface

Rib

An open stroke along existing surface, proposing extra structural emphasis — a splice line, a reinforced bay boundary — rather than a new opening or a new form.

Same as the aperture: raycast against the live mesh at every point. Only makes sense drawn onto something that exists, so it is unavailable on the blank site.

The resolution to the 2D-vs-3D tension, stated plainly: a spine drawn in open air is lifted by an inferred plane; a spine drawn on real geometry is already 3D the instant it lands, because the surface it touches is. The concept grows more confident and less inference-dependent the longer a session runs.

where it sits

The creation act and the refinement act, on one object.

Spine drawing is not a second tool beside sculpting. It sits further toward playdough than sculpt does, because it starts from nothing — a proposal, not an already-buildable thing. Both hand off to the same numbers panel on demand.

one session · seven frames

Blank site to finished arch, and taking a wrong read back.

Each frame is a real state of the loop, drawn in the product's own palette — ink line work, one olive accent for anything alive, amber only where a strut nears its fabrication limit.

Ground plane, a faint envelope guide, a compass mark. Nothing else. One quiet instruction.

A spine node — now an ordinary shell node — pulled outward with the sculpt influence bubble. One strut shifts amber as it nears its fab band.

Scrub back

The handle sits one tick before the aperture; it is ghosted out, stats dimmed. A wrong read is just a gesture you scrub past. No dialog anywhere.

honest boundaries

What is faux, and the questions still open.

The scope was a concept, not the inference engine. Naming the seams plainly is the point — it is what makes this safe to show and build against.

Genuinely faux, named plainly

  • The classifier. Infer sketches the kind of signal available — arc length, contact surface, curvature sign, closure — not a designed model. A real system needs one tuned against real messy strokes.
  • The confidence threshold. What decides whether alternate-reading chips appear is asserted for the concept, not derived. It needs tuning against strokes from real people, which do not exist yet.
  • The resolve ramp. Wireframe to grain is a deliberate device translating one real decision into motion. It sits a step short of the "never fake computation" line, flagged rather than hidden.

Hard HCI questions surfaced, not solved

  • Confidently wrong. Chips only help when the engine knows it is unsure. A confident wrong read depends entirely on inference quality, out of scope here.
  • Correction at scale. Scrub-undo takes back one spine. Recategorizing three of ten at once likely needs a persistent, editable log of what the engine read each spine as.
  • Gate fatigue. 500ms window and 2s resolve are starting points. Even a silent gate can feel naggy if the resolve is loud every single time. Needs a feel-pass once built.
  • Discoverability. Grab-and-pull has an everyday precedent; "draw a spine" has none. One instruction line is the proposed minimum, not a confirmed answer.
